The k-NN algorithm

Assumption: Similar Inputs have similar outputs
Classification rule: For a test input xx, assign the most common label amongst its k most similar training inputs

Neighbors' labels are 2×2×⊕ and 1×1×⊖ and the result is ⊕.


Formal (and borderline incomprehensible) definition of k-NN:
  • Test point: xx
  • Define the set of the kk nearest neighbors of xx as SxSx. Formally SxSx is defined as Sx⊆DSx⊆D s.t. |Sx|=k|Sx|=k and ∀(x′,y′)∈D∖Sx∀(x′,y′)∈D∖Sx,
    dist(x,x′)≥max(x′′,y′′)∈Sxdist(x,x′′),dist(x,x′)≥max(x″,y″)∈Sxdist(x,x″),
    (i.e. every point in DD but not in SxSx is at least as far away from xx as the furthest point in SxSx). We can then define the classifier h()h() as a function returning the most common label in SxSx:
    h(x)=mode({y′′:(x′′,y′′)∈Sx}),h(x)=mode({y″:(x″,y″)∈Sx}),
    where mode(⋅)mode(⋅) means to select the label of the highest occurrence.
    • (Hint: In case of a draw, a good solution is to return the result of kk-NN with smaller kk)

    What distance function should we use?

    The k-nearest neighbor classifier fundamentally relies on a distance metric. The better that metric reflects label similarity, the better the classified will be. The most common choice is the Minkowski distance
    dist(x,z)=(∑r=1d|xr−zr|p)1/p.dist(x,z)=(∑r=1d|xr−zr|p)1/p.

    Quiz#1: This distance definition is pretty general and contains many well-known distances as special cases:
  • p=1p=1: Manhattan Distance (l1l1-norm)
  • p=2p=2: Euclidean Distance (l2l2-norm)
  • p→∞p→∞: Maximum Norm
  • p→0p→0: (not well defined)

    1-NN Convergence Proof

    Cover and Hart 1967[1]: As n→∞n→∞, the 11-NN error is no more than twice the error of the Bayes Optimal classifier. (Similar guarantees hold for k>1k>1.)

    In the limit case, the test point and its nearest neighbor are identical. There are exactly two cases when a misclassification can occur: when the test point and its nearest neighbor have different labels.     Rescue to the curse: Data may lie in low dimensional subspace or on sub-manifolds. Example: natural images (digits, faces).

    k-NN summary

  • kk- NN is a simple and effective classifier if distances reliably reflect a semantically meaningful notion of the dissimilarity. (It becomes truly competitive through metric learning)
  • As n→∞n→∞, kk-NN becomes provably very accurate, but also very slow.
  • As d→∞d→∞, the curse of dimensionality becomes a concern.
